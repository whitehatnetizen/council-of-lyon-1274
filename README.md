# The Lyon Manuscript

A creative writing project exploring historical fiction through the lens of medieval documentary styles.

---

## Concept

This project takes a single speculative fiction premise and refracts it through multiple authentic medieval voices: the chronicle, the council record, and the scholastic lecture.

**The premise:** Thomas Aquinas did not die on the road to the Council of Lyon in 1274. He arrived — but what arrived was no longer entirely the man who had set out from Naples. Something happened to him in December 1273, during the elevation of the Host, that made his eight million words of theological writing feel like "straw." At Lyon, summoned to refute the errors of the Greeks, he instead delivered a speech that ended the schism — not through theological argument, but through revelation of something that made unity a "mutual defensive necessity."

The project explores themes of faith, knowledge, the limits of language, cosmic horror, and the gap between hearing *about* God and *seeing* God.

## The Documents

### Core Narrative

**`StoryText/Thomas Aquinas.txt`**

The seed text. A modern-voice telling of the premise: Thomas arrives at Lyon, delivers a speech that begins as expected but rapidly becomes something else, and reads from his manuscript in words older than words. Includes the chilling footnote about the Dominican motto changing to *Nescire* ("To not know") and the *Summa Theologica* being placed on the Index of Forbidden Books.

---

### Stylistic Retellings

**`StoryText/MP_commentary.md`** — *Matthew Paris Style*

The same events retold as a 13th-century English chronicler would record them: annalistic structure, editorial asides, vivid anecdotes, Protestant-before-Protestantism suspicion of Rome. Matthew Paris was a Benedictine monk at St Albans who wrote gossipy, opinionated chronicles. His voice is narrative, judgmental, and fascinated by portents.

**`StoryText/BN_commentary.md`** — *Brevis Nota Style*

The same events as dry conciliar documentation: formal, impersonal, focused on procedure and canonical consequences. The *Brevis Nota* was the official record of council proceedings. Its voice strips away personality and presents only what can be administratively acknowledged — which makes what it *cannot* acknowledge all the more unsettling.

**`StoryText/TA_speech.md`** — *Thomas Aquinas Lecture Style*

Thomas's actual speech at Lyon, reconstructed in authentic scholastic form: a scriptural *thema* (Job 42:5-6), announced divisions, tripartite analysis, chains of patristic authority. The structure begins orderly and progressively collapses as Thomas's mind fragments under the weight of what he has seen. The formal machinery of scholasticism grinds on even as the man operating it dissolves.

## Historical Context

**Thomas Aquinas (1225–1274)** was a Dominican friar and the most influential theologian in Western Christianity. His *Summa Theologica* remains foundational to Catholic philosophy. In December 1273, he had an experience during Mass that caused him to stop writing entirely. When urged to continue, he reportedly said: "I cannot, because all that I have written seems like straw to me now." He died in March 1274 while traveling to the Council of Lyon.

**The Council of Lyon (1274)** was convened by Pope Gregory X to reunify the Eastern (Orthodox) and Western (Catholic) churches, which had been in schism since 1054. Thomas was summoned to aid in convincing the Greeks of their "errors" — he had written *Contra Errores Graecorum* specifically for this purpose. He never arrived.

**The Great Schism** centered on theological disputes (particularly the *filioque* — whether the Holy Spirit proceeds from the Father alone or from the Father *and the Son*) and questions of papal authority. The churches remain divided to this day.

This project asks: *What if Thomas arrived? What if what he had seen in December made theological disputation feel not just inadequate, but dangerous? What if the "straw" was not humility but horror?*

## Notes on Method

Each retelling required:
1. Studying authentic source texts in the target style
2. Identifying characteristic features (structure, voice, vocabulary, concerns)
3. Creating a style guide documenting these features
4. Rewriting the narrative while preserving meaning but transforming voice

The goal is not pastiche but inhabitation — to write *as* Matthew Paris or the council secretary would write, with their assumptions, limitations, and blindnesses. What a chronicler notices differs from what a bureaucrat records differs from what a theologian argues. The same truth looks different through each lens.
